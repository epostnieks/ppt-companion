Our current highly centralized, specialized, and globalized industrial food system has a high system beta, making it vulnerable to large, systemic shocks like pandemics, climate-induced crop failures, or supply chain collapses. A more diversified, regional, and resilient food system, one that relies less on CAFOs and more on regenerative practices and local supply chains, would have a *lower* system beta. It might seem less "efficient" in a narrow economic sense, but it would be far more robust and less prone to collapse. Understanding system beta guides us to de-risk our food system by diversifying, decentralizing, and building resilience at local and regional levels.

ALEX: So, the Confinement Premium tells us *why* industrial meat is artificially dominant, and system beta tells us *how fragile* that dominance makes us. Both point to a fundamental mispricing and misallocation of resources.

JORDAN: Exactly. They provide the analytical rigor to back up what many intuitively feel is wrong with the system. They move the conversation from moral arguments to hard economic realities, which is often what's needed to spur policy action.
