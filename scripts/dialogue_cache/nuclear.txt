JORDAN: That's a crucial question, Alex, and it gets to the heart of SAPM's innovation. It's a multi-step, multidisciplinary process. First, you start with the **Private Payoff**. This is the traditional economic measure: the revenue, profit, or market capitalization generated by the industry or activity. It's what the market values.

ALEX: Okay, so far, so traditional.

JORDAN: Second, and this is where SAPM diverges, you systematically identify all significant **Externalities** – both positive and negative – across the entire lifecycle and value chain of the industry. We categorize these into channels, like the ones we discussed for nuclear or fossil fuels. This requires deep domain expertise in environmental science, social science, public health, geopolitics, and engineering.

ALEX: That sounds like a massive undertaking.

JORDAN: It is. Third, for each externality channel, we perform a **Quantification and Monetization (or Welfare Unit Conversion)**. This is the most challenging and innovative part. Some externalities have direct, measurable economic costs, like healthcare costs from pollution or property damage from climate events. Those are relatively straightforward.

ALEX: But for the less direct stuff...

JORDAN: For less direct impacts, like the value of a clean environment or biodiversity, we use established economic methodologies like shadow pricing, hedonic pricing, or contingent valuation surveys. ALEX: And the really abstract stuff?

JORDAN: For truly non-market goods, or for impacts that are difficult to put a direct dollar figure on – like the Persistence Floor of nuclear waste, or the long-term impact of species extinction – we use physical metrics and convert them into "welfare units." This involves establishing equivalence scales. For instance, a certain level of radiotoxicity over a million years might be assigned a welfare burden equivalent to a certain number of lost DALYs – Disability-Adjusted Life Years – or a percentage reduction in future economic potential. This is where the physics and ecological modeling become critical.

ALEX: And the impossibility theorem for nuclear waste...

JORDAN: ...it, uh, defines a non-negotiable welfare burden that simply cannot be reduced below a certain floor, regardless of economic will. And then, there's intergenerational discounting. This is another critical difference from traditional economics. SAPM typically uses a much lower or even zero intergenerational discount rate for long-term, irreversible harms.

ALEX: So, traditional economics often makes future costs seem less significant...

JORDAN: Exactly. SAPM argues that irreversible harms to future generations should not be discounted at rates that essentially make them disappear from current calculations.

ALEX: That makes sense.

JORDAN: Fourth, you **Aggregate System Payoff**. You sum up the private payoff and all the quantified externalities (positive and negative) to get the **Total System Payoff**. This is the true net benefit or cost to society. Finally, you calculate the **System Beta**. This is the ratio of the negative externalities (or welfare destruction) to the private payoff.

ALEX: So, Beta = (Absolute Value of Negative Externalities) / Private Payoff.

JORDAN: Right. If Beta > 1, it's a "Hollow Win." If 0 < Beta < 1, it's a "Net Positive Value" industry, like existing nuclear. If Beta = 0 or approaches zero, meaning minimal externalities, and private payoff is positive, it's a "True Win."

JORDAN: You're right, there's always an element of estimation when dealing with complex systems and long time horizons. JORDAN: No, it's about the fundamental structural realities. JORDAN: That's perhaps the biggest hurdle. The industries identified as "Hollow Wins" are often incredibly powerful, with vast financial resources and lobbying power. They benefit immensely from the current system of externalizing costs and will fiercely resist any framework that seeks to internalize those costs. Politicians who rely on their donations or who represent regions dependent on these industries will also resist.

JORDAN: Exactly. Lack of a global governance mechanism. Many of these externalities, like climate change, are global. Implementing SAPM effectively requires international cooperation and agreement on common metrics and policy responses, which is notoriously difficult. And the complexity and communication. While the core concept of SAPM is elegant, the underlying calculations can be complex. Communicating these findings effectively to the public and policymakers, in a way that is understandable and actionable, is a continuous challenge.
